Master Mind

(From The Playboy Winner's Guide to Board Games by Jon Freeman, Chicago: Playboy Press, 1979. ISBN #0-872-16562-0.)

Unless you've spent the last five years in exile on the island of Fernando Poo, you must have seen or heard of Master Mind (Invicta Plastics Ltd., USA), the most successful game of recent times; it has sold tens of millions of copies in half a dozen versions and inspired a multitude of derivatives and imitations.

While not at all complicated, Master Mind is (unlike chess or go or Othello) asymmetrical: that is, the two players involved do not begin with identical positions and goals. One, the codemaker, drawing from a group of pegs of six different colors, sets up a hidden "code" of four pegs. His opponent, the codebreaker, then attempts to guess the codeâ€”to duplicate it on the boardâ€”first at random and then by using logic to deduce the answer from clues given by the codemaker. These clues are simply a variation of a guessing game known to every child: a formalized way of saying, "You're cold ... you're warm ... you're hot." Every time the codebreaker makes a guessâ€”by placing a row of "code pegs" he hopes will duplicate the hidden codeâ€”the codemaker places, next to the row, a black "key peg" for every code peg that is the right color and in the right position (that is, that corresponds exactly to one element of the code) and a white key peg for each code peg that is of the right color but not in the right place. The codemaker never states which key pegs go with which code pegsâ€”that's something the codebreaker has to figure out along the way.

After the codebreaker has deduced the code, the two players trade places and roles. Whoever guesses the other's code in the fewest number of rows wins the round. Alternatively, players may keep a running total over any number of rounds. While some people, particularly under the stress of formal competition, may take forever, a complete two-game round should take less than half an hour, and fifteen minutes is more typical. With practice, you should be able to guess the code in an average of five rows or less.[¹]

Since Master Mind is essentially a game of codebreaking, the analysis that follows concentrates on the inferences that can be drawn from clues and the kinds of guesses that should be made. The first point to remember is that there are two kinds of information given by key pegs: their number and their color. Often, the former allows clearer deductions than the latter, as we shall see in the following two examples. The first is taken from The Official Master Mind Handbook by Leslie H. Ault. It will serve as an example of what not to do.

From the information of the first set of key pegs, the codebreaker hypothesized that the brown, red, and green code pegs corresponded to the black, white, and white key pegs for the first row. So, for the second guess, brown stayed where it was; red and green moved; and blue was brought in to try in the place of the (temporarily) rejected orange. Wrong: Since the number of key pegs decreased, orange (the color that left) must be in the code, and the new color, blue, must be out. On the other hand, two of the three original "test" colors (i.e., brown, red, and green) are correct, and brown still might be in the right spot. Since that left the fourth peg unaccounted for, the codebreaker brought in the last color, yellow; returned orange (but to a different position); discarded blue (permanently) and green (tentatively); moved red again; and retained brown in its slot.

Wrong again, and this time the absence of a black key peg means that brown has to go (it might still be in the code, but not in the #1 slot). That means that the black key pegs in the first two rows referred to two different code pegs (either two different colors or the same color but in two different positionsâ€”indicating a "double"). Which ones? There is no way to tellâ€”not yet. However, the number of key pegs again tells us something. There were two color changes between the second and third rows; from our previous conclusions, though, we know that one switchâ€”replacing the blue with the orangeâ€”was correct. Since the number of key pegs (and, therefore, correct code pegs) remained the same, the other switchâ€”replacing the green with the yellowâ€”must be incorrect. So green is in, and both "newcomers" (the two that weren't part of the first row), yellow and blue, go to the reject pile. Since one of the original four colors is incorrect (either red or brown, we can now see), there is obviously a "double" in the code: that is, one of the three colors in the code is duplicated.

Examine the codebreaker's fourth row carefully. By guessing red for the double, he maximized his informational input.[²] Two key pegs would have meant that brown, not red, was in the code, but the three key pegs he actually got indicated his colors were right; only the double was wrong. Note also the elementary fact that the fourth guess was a possible result: The placement of the pegs was in agreement with all previous clues.

It was, of course, wrong, but after that guess, a solution on the fifth row was, if not obvious, at least inevitable. Compare only the first and fourth rows. The black key peg cannot refer to the orange code peg (that is, orange cannot occupy the #4 slot), because that leaves no place (no black pegs for any of the other positions) for red. On the other hand, the black key peg in the first row can't be red's, either, for if it is, what can correspond to the black key peg in the fourth row? Not orange (which was sitting in the same position in the first row and couldn't have a black peg), not green (which is sitting in red's slot), and not red, which isn't a duplicate. That, obviously, leaves only green (brown is out, don't forget) for the #3 slot in the first row. Interestingly, that means the black peg in the second row corresponds to a green code peg, also (red would be stealing green's spot, and the other two colors in the row aren't in the code). That gives us the duplicate and its dual position, and the fourth row (again) proves red must be in the #1 position (no other color could deserve a black peg). That leaves orange for the #2 spot, and the code is solved.

This example demonstrates a key point in code solving: Find a hypothesis that fits the facts, and stick with it until proved wrong. Otherwise, you'll get lost trying to juggle seventeen alternate possibilities simultaneously. There is a second point, also: After the second row, use only guesses that are possible solutions. This may seem obvious, but careful codebreakers do not always follow it.[³]

Most beginning Master Mind players open much like the two codebreakers seen so far: The second row is dependent on the results of the first and the whim of the moment. Most experienced players, however, use a planned opening: a two-row pattern played (almost) regardless of the codemaker's response to the first. (The second row thus becomes, often, a limited use of the "informative impossible.") While I am not wholly convinced that a planned opening is theoretically superior to the standard intuitive development, it does have a couple of practical advantages. To a definite degree, a planned opening provides the same kind of information every time; you know what to expect from it, what to look for. Second, it seems to allow you to draw clearer inferences faster than you normally do with a more haphazard opening, which too often involves juggling dozens of possibilities for three rounds or more. Rather than a fuzzy picture that suddenly becomes clear (like a photograph being developed), the planned opening allows (or forces) you to treat the code as a jigsaw puzzle put together one step at a time. For most people, this is probably easier to learn.

I have concentrated on the techniques of codebreaking because Master Mind is very definitely the codebreaker's game. Codemaker tactics are limited to picking a fairly random group of three or four colors. Odd codes with double doubles or a triple play may confuse your opponent momentarily, but any of a large number of guesses will reveal the deceit, and thereafter a codebreaker has only a small number of possibilities to contend with. Like backgammonâ€”only more soâ€”Master Mind is easy enough to learn and quick enough to play to entice almost anyone to try it. My own suggestion is to begin with Mini Master Mind: while having fewer rows (six) than the standard ten, it is just as complicated as the regular game and can be kept as a pocket or travel set if you "outgrow" it. From there you can push on to the more elaborate and more flexible Super Master Mind (nÃ©e Deluxe Master Mind), which includes eight colors (or nine, with blanks) in five holes and is considerably more complicated. If you're still not sated, you can try sinking your teeth into Grand Master Mind, which mixes shapes and colors and has three kinds of key pegs. (But don't say you weren't warned.)

One of Master Mind's greatest advantages is that this flexibility allows two players of considerably different skill levels to compete on fairly even terms. The better player, for instance, could be forbidden to make codes with duplicates, while his opponent suffered no such prohibition. On a more sophisticated level (and where the gap in skill was greater), with Super Master Mind, one side could use more colors (or blanks) and/or more holes than the other. With a bit of experimentation, you should be able to come up with a handicap that allows you and your partner to guess each other's code, on average, in the same number of rows.

A modern classic, Master Mind deserves a spot (or two) on everyone's gameshelf.
